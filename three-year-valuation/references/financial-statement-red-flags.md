# Financial Statement Red-Flag Checklist

Use this reference for annual-report review, financial-quality review, or any deep company analysis. Always prefer consolidated statements. Parent-company-only statements are usually secondary unless the holding-company structure itself is the issue.

## 1. Operating Rule

Financial statements are primarily a rejection tool. A company does not need to be proven fraudulent to be rejected; material unexplained doubt is enough.

Treat each item as green, yellow, red, or unknown:

- Green: data is normal and corroborated.
- Yellow: abnormal but plausibly explained; watch closely.
- Red: material, unexplained, or repeated abnormality; stop before valuation unless the user explicitly asks for an illustrative exercise.
- Unknown: required data is missing; do not invent it.

## 2. Balance Sheet

### Cash and Debt

Red flags:

- Cash is far below short-term debt or upcoming maturities.
- Cash appears abundant while the company borrows at high rates.
- Restricted cash or other monetary funds are large without clear explanation.
- Interest income divided by average cash is far below a normal deposit yield.
- Balance-sheet cash does not reconcile with cash-flow statement cash equivalents and notes.

Cross-check:

- Read notes for restricted versus unrestricted cash.
- Compare interest income with average monetary funds.
- Compare cash, short-term debt, interest expense, and maturity schedule.

### Receivables and Contract Assets

Red flags:

- Receivables grow much faster than revenue.
- Receivable days are worse than peers or deteriorating quickly.
- Long-aged receivables are large or bad-debt provisions are unusually light.
- Revenue rises while cash collection is weak.
- Receivables fall while other receivables, prepayments, or related-party balances jump.

Cross-check:

- Read aging table, bad-debt policy, top customers, and related-party notes.
- Compare sales cash received with revenue including tax where relevant.

### Inventory

Red flags:

- Inventory grows much faster than revenue or cost of goods sold.
- Gross margin improves while inventory builds without a business explanation.
- Obsolescence-prone inventory has low impairment provisions.
- Biological, commodity, fashion, technology, or project inventory is hard to verify.
- Inventory accounting methods change and improve reported profit.

Cross-check:

- Read inventory composition, impairment provisions, costing method, and turnover.
- Compare inventory, gross margin, revenue growth, and production capacity.

### Production Assets, Construction, and Intangibles

Red flags:

- Construction in progress is large and does not transfer to fixed assets.
- Depreciation or amortization policy changes increase profit.
- Long-term deferred expenses are large or rising.
- Goodwill rises after expensive acquisitions or is tested with vague assumptions.
- Large impairments appear after prior aggressive expansion.
- R&D capitalization is high or rising without clear commercial proof.

Cross-check:

- Read project schedules, impairment assumptions, asset useful lives, and acquisition disclosures.
- Compare capex, depreciation, capacity, revenue, and management expansion claims.

### Other Assets and Related Parties

Red flags:

- Other receivables or prepayments are large.
- Related-party transactions are material, opaque, or priced oddly.
- Guarantees, pledged assets, lawsuits, or commitments create off-balance-sheet pressure.
- Financial assets and fair-value changes contribute materially to profit.

Cross-check:

- Read related-party notes, guarantee/pledge disclosures, fair-value hierarchy, and contingencies.

## 3. Income Statement

Red flags:

- Operating profit is not the main source of profit.
- Non-recurring gains, subsidies, asset sales, or fair-value gains explain much of net profit.
- Gross margin moves sharply without matching price, mix, cost, or scale evidence.
- Impairment losses are reversed opportunistically.
- Selling, R&D, or admin expense ratios drop suddenly without a credible efficiency driver.
- Tax rate is unusually low or volatile without explanation.

Cross-check:

- Compare margins with peers and history.
- Reconcile reported net profit with recurring operating profit and cash conversion.

## 4. Cash Flow Statement

Red flags:

- Operating cash flow persistently trails net profit.
- Operating cash flow is negative for a mature non-financial company.
- Sales cash collection is far below revenue.
- Free cash flow is weak because maintenance capex or working capital consumes profit.
- Financing cash flow repeatedly fills operating or investment cash gaps.
- Dividends or buybacks are not supported by real free cash flow.

Cross-check:

- Compare net profit, operating cash flow, capex, dividends, debt issuance, and cash balances across at least three years when available.

## 5. Notes and Management Discussion

Read notes before accepting unusual numbers. Prioritize cash restrictions, receivable aging, inventory provisions, fixed-asset changes, intangible assets, goodwill impairment, R&D capitalization, leases, employee compensation, government grants, related parties, contingencies, and audit opinions.

Management discussion should be cross-checked against numbers:

- Past targets versus actual results.
- Capacity claims versus fixed assets, construction in progress, and production/sales data.
- Market-share claims versus company growth and industry growth.
- Cost-control claims versus gross margin and expense ratios.
- R&D claims versus capitalized versus expensed R&D and product outcomes.

## 6. High-Risk Contexts

Use stricter skepticism when:

- Revenue, costs, or assets are difficult to verify externally.
- The industry depends heavily on subsidies, long projects, biological assets, rapid technology change, or aggressive capitalization.
- The company has frequent acquisitions, repeated restatements, qualified audit opinions, major related-party dealings, or controlling-shareholder funding stress.

## 7. Stop Rules

Stop before valuation, or mark valuation as illustrative only, when:

- Any major red flag remains unexplained.
- Three or more yellow flags point in the same direction.
- Audit opinion, restatement, related-party, cash, or debt issues create credibility doubts.
- Management claims repeatedly fail numerical cross-checks.
- The statements are too complex to understand within the available data.
